Problem Overview¶

The dengue fever, as the name suggests, is a mosquito-borne disease caused by the dengue viruses. The symptoms of dengue fever include, as listed on WebMD:

  • sudden, high fever
  • severe headaches
  • pain behind the eyes
  • severe joint and muscle pain
  • fatigue
  • nausea
  • vomiting
  • skin rash
  • mild bleeding

The symptoms are sometimes mild and mistaken for flu. Serious problems can develop, however, if there was lack of early recognition and prompt supportive treatment.

The dengue fever has become a global problem since the 1950s and is now a leading cause of illness and death in the tropics and subtropics. According to Centers of Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), there are not yet any vaccines to prevent dengue fever.

Dengue fever is spread through the bite of female mosquito (Aedes aegypti) when the mosquito takes blood of a infected person and bites a healthy person within a week. Based on information given on World Health Organization's website, dengue outbreaks can occur anytime as long as there are mosquitoes alive. Yet regions with high humidity and high temperature usually have more likehood of dengue outbreaks.

In this project, we explore the dengue fever dataset and try to predict the number of dengue cases based on environmental variables. The dataset contains different aspects of data for two cities: San Juan (sj) and Iquitos (iq). Aside from date indicators (week_start_date), NDVI indexes (ndvi_se, ndvi_sw, ndvi_ne, ndvi_nw) are also given, which measure the density of vegetation on a patch of land and can be used as an indon icator of agricultural drought. These indexes are relevant because mosquitoes lay eggs in water and the eggs need water to hatch as well. If a region is experiencing emtreme drought, chances of mosquitoes surviving in that area becomes low, hence reducing the spread rate of dengue fever. There is also detailed climate information provided in the dataset, coming from three different sources. station_max_temp_c, station_min_temp_c, station_avg_temp_c, station_precip_mm, and station_diur_temp_rng_c are measurements from National Ocean and Atmospheric Administration's (NOAA) Global Historical Climatology Network (GHCN) daily climate data weather station, which shows data about maximum temperature, minimun temperature, average temperature, total precipitation, and diurnal temperature range corresondingly. precipitation_amt_mm data is generated by PERSIANN-CDR, an artifical neural network that provides daily rainfall estimates, and represents the total precipitation of a given region at a given time. And finally, the rest of the data come from the NCEP Climate Forecast System Reanalysis (CFSR), which is known for its accuracy of estimation, where reanalysis_sat_precip_amt_mm represents total precipitation, reanalysis_dew_point_temp_k for mean dew point temperature, reanalysis_air_temp_k for mean air temperature, reanalysis_relative_humidity_percent for mean relative humidity, reanalysis_specific_humidity_g_per_kg for mean specific humidity, reanalysis_precip_amt_kg_per_m2 for total precipitation, reanalysis_max_air_temp_k for maximum air temperature, reanalysis_min_air_temp_k for minimum air temperature, reanalysis_avg_temp_k for average air temperature, and finally reanalysis_tdtr_k for diurnal temperature range. Information about temperature, precipitation, and humidity is largely needed because, as explained earlier, mosquitoes live in warm environment and rely on water to lay eggs and hatch. It is very likely to have a positive relationship between warm temperature, high humidity, and more dangue cases reported.

New Variable¶

We created a new variable called lag_max which is generated calculateind the differece between the maximum temperatures (station_max_temp_c) of two consective week, because we wanted to know about how change of climate may affect the amount of mosquitoes coming in (aka cases of dengue fever). There are a few reasons why climate change may increase Dengue risk. First, the incubation period of the virus shortens in warmer temperatures, which means a mosquito doesn’t have to survive as long to have a chance of becoming infectious. Second, the range of the mosquito is increasing due to global warming. In general, climate is a key factor controlling where a species can live. When climate changes, individuals move to stay in a suitable habitat. In this sense, we created this new new variable to keep track of changes in climate.

What made the distrbution of the two cities so different? Interestingly, San Juan has a tropical monsoon climate. Rainfall is well-distributed throughout the year, but January, February, and March are the driest. This explains why cases of dengue decrease during the first quarter of the year (mosquitoes don't like dry weather) and increase from week 17 (April). On the other hand, Iquitos has tropical rainforest climate, where there is constant rainfall throughout the year, without a distinct dry season. This is why the curve for Iquitos is a lot more flat.

Statistical Modeling¶

In [14]:
var = total.total_cases.var()
mean = total.total_cases.mean()

Looking at the previous section about variables correlated to our model and the dataset, we decided that either a Poisson or Negative Binomial Regression model would best fit for our distribution, since our desired outcomes are discrete counts (integers).

To determine which model to use, we first computed the variance of total_cases, which is about 1905.6. We then check the mean of total_cases, which is only about 24.7.

At this point, it became very clear that the Negative Binomial Regression model should be used. The variance is way greater than the mean, which suggests over-dispersion. The negative binomial distribution has one more parameter in its function than the Poisson regression, which adjusts the variance independently from the mean.

Our Models¶

We then created models for the San Juan data frame and Iquitos data frame correspondingly.

sj_model = smf.glm(formula='total_cases ~ reanalysis_precip_amt_kg_per_m2', data=sj, family=sm.families.NegativeBinomial()).fit()
iq_model = smf.glm(formula='total_cases ~ reanalysis_precip_amt_kg_per_m2', data=iq, family=sm.families.NegativeBinomial()).fit()
sj['sj_predictions'] = sj_model.predict()
iq['iq_predictions'] = iq_model.predict()
